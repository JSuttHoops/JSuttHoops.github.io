The NBA feels awfully angry lately, don't you think?

It seems as though fans, players, and coaches are in agony about some part of the game every single night now. Fans and coaches are complaining about referees, players are complaining about getting fined for complaining about said referees, and players are arguing with previous legends about the merits of their own careers:

Discourse isn't a new phenomenon in sports (nor is it in most forms of media). However, one of my recent reads got me thinking about just how angry the state of NBA fandom seems to be.

I just finished reading Narrative Economics by Robert J. Shiller, the recipient of the 2013 Nobel Prize for his work in the titular field, which appears to be still nascent despite his work. I highly recommend reading the book, but the gist of it is this: Economic crashes, booms, and major events do not always cause public psychological changes, but the other way around.

For example, the 2007-2009 Financial Crisis, according to Shiller, can be at least partially traced to the idea of "homeownership" as a patriotic value that every person in America should strive for, regardless of economic status. This psychological shift then led to astronomical home prices, which, when combined with the mortgage fiasco going on at the time, buckled the market.

I am oversimplifying it here, but it got me wondering: Are we really at an all-time high for NBA discourse? And if so, what does that say about the future of the league?

So let's start with that first question: Is this the most discourse we've ever seen in the NBA?

Analyzing Discourse

The first hurdle to clear is actually analyzing and quantifying "discourse" in the NBA. There are many ways you could go about it, but the one I settled on is this:

  • Grab all of the headlines of ESPN NBA articles going back to 2005-ish
  • Fill some gaps in ESPN's data via Google News, which will include other news outlets, to get a more rounded view of the media landscape
  • Use NLP (natural language processing) to analyze the sentiment of all headlines over time

Is it a perfect method? No, as it doesn't include physical newspapers, NBA Twitter discourse, or podcasts, but it's a good proxy for what the average NBA fan sees. ESPN is still the most popular sports outlet in the world, and therefore is the most influential.

I've also collected a few key terms that we can analyze individually if we so choose. Some of these terms include:

  • Referees/refs
  • Gambling/sportsbooks/betting
  • Flopping/foul-baiting
  • Fixing/cheating/rigged
  • Load-management

All of the above can then be used to create proxy scores for "anger" and "complaints," which usually follow each other but can diverge in certain instances. When you map it out over the course of the past two decades, you see a very clear trend: There is a more angry sentiment now in the media than we've ever seen, and it's by a wide margin:

Over 12% of articles (a massive number) have an angry sentiment involved, while around 4.5% (also a big number, though not as big) have direct complaints associated. The difference, of course, is that some headlines imply anger over flopping and foul-baiting, but others are simply "complaining" when it comes to things like gambling concerns.

When you break it out into individual aspects and smooth the data a little bit (to eliminate outliers, which are represented by the dots below), you'll see that the biggest increases in complaints/discourse have come from officiating and gambling/betting:

There's something obvious here: The anger and complaining has reached epidemic levels. And when you look at it empirically, this is the most discourse-heavy season that we've seen in the modern era:

So what is causing all of the discourse, you might ask? Let's take a look, and in the process, cover a very important term for where we're going: Flashbulb Moments.

What Are Flashbulb Moments?

In Shiller's book, a key point of emphasis is the Flashbulb Moment, which we can explain using an analogy from a different field. When you look at how diseases spread, epidemics can only happen if the recovery rate is lower than the contagion rate. If the recovery rate is higher than the contagion rate, everybody would have recovered prior to the epidemic becoming a problem, right? Well, replace "recovery rate" with "forgetting rate," which is how quickly a person forgets the discourse that has happened. The primary thing that affects the forgetting rate is a Flashbulb Moment. These are moments in time that are so prominent, so important, that they alter how people think about a subject for a time. In narrative economics, these Flashbulb Moments can look like a terrible event, like World War II or a stock market crash. In the NBA, it usually involves some sort of scandal.

All of these moments in time were Flashbulb Moments for the media and for fans, though of different levels of severity. The Donaghy betting scandal still has implications today for fan trust, while the NBA's first anti-flopping penalties, despite not truly being implemented, brought more attention the fact that flopping was an issue in the first place. You'll notice that much of this surrounds refereeing and gambling, which feels about right given what we all seem to be talking about right now. Plus, we've never seen this many Flashbulb Moments in such a short period of time. They're arriving faster, and there's more of them at once:

Why does that matter, you ask? Because it significantly lowers the forgetting rate. When a flashbulb moment occurs, discourse takes a decently long time to come back to normal levels: 61 days, to be exact.

One thing you may notice from the chart is that, usually, a few months before a Flashbulb Moment occurs, discourse has a small spike, which may imply that there is some sort of invisible buildup that's happening here. I won't dive deep into that spike in this piece, but it's worth noting, because you see reflections of that anomalous pre-Flashbulb Moment in Narrative Economics as a whole.

The other problem is that having multiple active Flashbulb Moments increases the level of discourse significantly. While one active Flashbulb moment causes the level of complaints to hit 2% (as opposed to around 1.4% for non-Flashbulb Moment periods), two or more cause 7 times as much anger and double the amount of complaints:

So what does all of this tell us? First, we are hitting an all-time high in the number of Flashbulb Moments, and despite there being no precedent, we can confidently say that it will take longer to forget than other times in NBA history. There are significant complaints around gambling (which is often turning into anger from a certain fan perspective), referee discourse is spiking, and I would suspect that trust in the NBA's integrity is at a low not seen since the Donaghy era.

If this continues, one of two things will happen:

  • Fans will slowly stop watching due to a lack of trust in the league's veracity
  • The NBA will try to stop the Flashbulb Moment by disciplining referees, limiting gambling, and/or solving the Kawhi Leonard scandal

The second option would likely be another Flashbulb Moment in and of itself, but a positive one that could stave off some of the effects of the previous ones. You want to create a Flashbulb Moment that's positive so that the forgetting rate of such positive actions goes down.

There is significant pressure on the NBA to do something here. The integrity of the league is at stake more than ever before, and there are many connections you could make between what's happening right now and, forgive me for my exaggeration, but you'll get the point, events like the French Revolution. Eventually, when the forgetting rate is too high for negative events, and trust takes a hit, fans, players, and coaches will revolt.

It doesn't seem like many people are talking about the metanarrative of what discourse means for the NBA. Instead, we like to focus on the fact that the Media is driving clicks via the discourse, and we say things like "The NBA needs to fix its refereeing problem," which is true but unhelpful in the grand scheme of things. What the NBA needs to fix is its forgetting rate, and that will only happen by counterbalancing it with positive Flashbulb Moments.

Otherwise, the matchup between Victor Wembanyama and Rudy Gobert may not be the only metaphor we use the French Revolution for.
